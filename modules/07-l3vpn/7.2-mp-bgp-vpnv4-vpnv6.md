# 7.2 — MP-BGP for VPNv4/VPNv6

## Overview

> MP-BGP is the control plane that makes L3VPN scale. Without it, every PE would need a full mesh of GRE tunnels or direct peering with every other PE for every VPN — the kind of architecture that makes experienced engineers twitch. MP-BGP VPNv4/VPNv6 address families carry customer prefixes + labels across the provider backbone over a single iBGP session, decoupled from the data plane entirely. This section goes deep on the BGP machinery: how VPN prefixes are encoded, how route targets flow through the RR hierarchy, how next-hop resolution actually works, and why getting label allocation wrong can melt your control plane. Section 7.1 showed you the architecture. This section shows you the engine.

> - how next-hop, path attributes, and policy drive best-path decisions

## Theory

### VPNv4 and VPNv6 Address Families

Standard BGP (AFI 1, SAFI 1) carries IPv4 unicast. It knows nothing about overlapping address spaces. MP-BGP (RFC 4760) extends BGP to carry arbitrary address families via the `MP_REACH_NLRI` and `MP_UNREACH_NLRI` path attributes.

L3VPN defines two key address families:

| Address Family | AFI | SAFI | Carries | NLRI Format |
|---------------|-----|------|---------|-------------|
| VPNv4 Unicast | 1 | 128 | IPv4 VPN routes | 8-byte RD + 4-byte IPv4 prefix |
| VPNv6 Unicast | 2 | 128 | IPv6 VPN routes | 8-byte RD + 16-byte IPv6 prefix |
| VPNv4 Multicast | 1 | 129 | IPv4 VPN multicast | Rarely used directly; modern MVPN uses MCAST-VPN NLRI (AFI 1, SAFI 5) per RFC 6514 |

**NLRI encoding** (VPNv4 example):

```
+------------------+------------------------------+
| RD (8 bytes)     | IPv4 Prefix (variable, ≤4B) |
| 65000:100        | 10.1.1.0/24                  |
+------------------+------------------------------+
= VPNv4 NLRI: 65000:100:10.1.1.0/24
```

The RD is prepended to the customer prefix to create a globally unique NLRI, even when two customers use the same 10.0.0.0/8. This is the entire reason RDs exist — uniqueness in the BGP table, nothing more.

### MP_REACH_NLRI Encoding for VPN Routes

When a PE advertises a VPN route, the UPDATE message contains:

```
BGP UPDATE:
  Path Attributes:
    ORIGIN: IGP
    AS_PATH: (empty for iBGP)
    MP_REACH_NLRI:
      AFI: 1 (IPv4)
      SAFI: 128 (MPLS-labeled VPN)
      Next-Hop: PE loopback (12 bytes: 8-byte RD of zeros + 4-byte IPv4)
      NLRI: label(3 bytes) + RD(8 bytes) + prefix(variable)
    EXTENDED_COMMUNITIES:
      Route-Target: 65000:100
      (optional) SOO: 65000:1001   ← Site of Origin, prevents loops for multi-homed CEs (see 7.1)
    MPLS Label: allocated by advertising PE
```

Key details most engineers miss:

1. **The label is IN the NLRI**, not a separate attribute. It's encoded as a 3-byte field prepended to the RD+prefix. This is why the NLRI looks weird in packet captures.

2. **Next-hop encoding is 12 bytes** for VPNv4 (not 4). Per RFC 4364 §4.3.4, the next-hop is encoded as 8-byte RD (all zeros) + 4-byte IPv4 address = 12 bytes. Wireshark shows this as `0:0:10.0.0.1`. A 16-byte IPv4-mapped-IPv6 encoding (`::ffff:10.0.0.1`) exists but only when the BGP session itself runs over IPv6 (RFC 8950). In standard IPv4-based SP deployments, expect 12 bytes.

3. **Extended communities carry Route Targets**. Regular BGP communities are 4 bytes. Extended communities (RFC 4360) are 8 bytes — big enough to hold ASN:value or IP:value RTs. They're transitive by default, meaning RRs propagate them even if the RR itself doesn't understand VPN.

### Route Target Constraint (RT-Constraint / RFC 4684)

In a large network, every PE receives every VPN route from every RR. If you have 5,000 VRFs across 200 PEs, but any given PE only participates in 50 VRFs, that PE is receiving and discarding routes for 4,950 VRFs it doesn't care about. This is pure waste — memory, CPU, bandwidth.

RT-Constraint (RFC 4684) fixes this. Each PE advertises the set of Route Targets it imports. The RR uses this information to filter VPN route advertisements — only sending routes to PEs that actually want them.

**How it works:**

```
PE1 imports RT 65000:100 and 65000:200
PE2 imports RT 65000:100 and 65000:300
PE3 imports RT 65000:200 and 65000:300

Without RT-Constraint:
  RR sends ALL VPN routes to ALL PEs → O(n²) waste

With RT-Constraint:
  PE1 advertises: "I want RT 65000:100, 65000:200"
  PE2 advertises: "I want RT 65000:100, 65000:300"
  PE3 advertises: "I want RT 65000:200, 65000:300"
  
  RR receives route for RT 65000:100 → sends to PE1 and PE2 only
  RR receives route for RT 65000:200 → sends to PE1 and PE3 only
```

RT-Constraint uses its own address family: **AFI 1, SAFI 132** (`ipv4 rt-filter` on IOS-XR, `route-target` family on Junos).

**The RR plays a critical role here.** If your RR doesn't support or isn't configured for RT-Constraint, it falls back to flooding everything. And if you enable RT-Constraint on PEs but not the RR, the PEs will advertise their RT membership, the RR will ignore it, and nothing changes. Both sides must participate.

**Default RT:** When RT-Constraint is first enabled, the RR withholds all VPN routes until PEs advertise their RT membership. This can cause a brief VPN outage during migration. Some implementations support a "default RT" that tells the RR to send everything until the PE explicitly subscribes. IOS-XR does this automatically during the negotiation window.

### Next-Hop Resolution and Recursive Lookup

VPN routes carried in MP-BGP have a next-hop that is the advertising PE's loopback. This next-hop must be resolved via the IGP/LDP/SR to determine the actual forwarding path. This two-stage resolution is called **recursive next-hop lookup**.

```
Customer route: 10.1.1.0/24
  VPN next-hop: 10.255.0.2 (PE2 loopback)
    Resolved via: IGP → 192.168.12.2 (P router interface)
      Label stack: [transport-label | VPN-label]
```

**Resolution table hierarchy:**

| Platform | VPN Next-Hop Resolves In | Why |
|----------|-------------------------|-----|
| IOS-XR | Default: uses global table, IGP routes | VPNv4 next-hop resolved in RIB |
| Junos | `inet.3` (MPLS-eligible routes only) | By design — only LDP/RSVP/SR routes in inet.3 |

This Junos behavior is critical. If you have a static route or directly connected route to the PE loopback but no LDP/SR label for it, Junos will NOT resolve the VPN next-hop. The route stays hidden. IOS-XR is more permissive — it'll resolve against any reachable route, which can lead to unlabeled VPN traffic hitting the core (bad).

**next-hop-self for VPNv4:**

On RRs, `next-hop-self` behavior differs from regular IPv4:

- **IOS-XR**: By default, the RR does NOT change the next-hop for VPNv4 reflected routes. This is correct — the VPN label was allocated by the originating PE, so the next-hop must point to that PE.
- **Junos**: Same behavior. RRs preserve next-hop for VPN families.
- **If you set next-hop-self on the RR for VPNv4**: The RR becomes the next-hop. The VPN label now points to the RR. The RR must have the VRF configured to forward VPN traffic. This is almost never what you want. Don't do it.

**Exception**: Inter-AS Option C uses next-hop-self on ASBRs, but that's a different scenario (Section 7.3).

### Label Allocation Modes

The PE allocates a VPN label for each VPN route (or group of routes) it advertises. The label is carried in the NLRI and tells the remote PE which VRF to deliver the packet to. How labels are allocated dramatically affects scale:

| Mode | Label Granularity | Memory | Forwarding Precision | Platform |
|------|-------------------|--------|---------------------|----------|
| Per-prefix | One label per VPN route | High | Exact match (route-specific) | IOS-XR default |
| Per-VRF | One label per VRF | Low | All routes in VRF share one label → IP lookup on egress PE | Junos with `vrf-table-label` |
| Per-CE | One label per CE neighbor | Medium | Per-neighbor granularity | IOS-XR configurable |
| Per-nexthop | One label per BGP next-hop in VRF | Medium | Groups routes by next-hop | Varies |

**Per-prefix** (IOS-XR default): Scales to millions of routes but consumes one LFIB entry per route per VRF. On hardware platforms with limited label space (older ASR9K line cards), this can hit limits around 1-2 million labels.

**Per-next-hop** (Junos default without `vrf-table-label`): Junos allocates one label per protocol next-hop in the VRF. Routes sharing the same BGP next-hop share a label. This is often conflated with per-prefix, but it's not — if 500 routes all have the same CE next-hop, they share one label.

**Per-VRF** (Junos `vrf-table-label`): The PE allocates a single label for the entire VRF. When a packet arrives with this label, the PE pops it and does a **second IP lookup** in the VRF routing table. This saves label space massively but adds one lookup. The real-world performance impact is negligible on modern hardware.

**Per-CE**: Compromise — one label per CE-facing interface. Packet arrives → pop label → forward out the specific CE interface. No IP lookup needed if there's only one CE route per interface.

**Choosing the right mode:**

- **< 100K routes across all VRFs**: Per-prefix is fine. Maximum forwarding efficiency.
- **> 100K routes or label-constrained hardware**: Per-VRF or per-CE. The second IP lookup is cheap.
- **SRv6 L3VPN**: Uses per-VRF (End.DT4/DT6 SID = VRF-level decapsulation).

### Route Reflector Design for VPN

RR design for VPN has different constraints than for Internet BGP:

**Memory**: A VPN RR holds routes from ALL VRFs. If you have 500 VRFs averaging 1,000 routes each, the RR carries 500,000 VPNv4 routes — even though no single PE holds more than a fraction. RRs are memory-intensive. Dedicated RR hardware is standard practice.

**Placement**: Three common designs:

```
Design 1: In-path RR (RR is also a P router)
  Pros: No extra hardware
  Cons: RR failure = forwarding + control plane impact

Design 2: Dedicated RR (out-of-path, not in forwarding plane)
  Pros: RR failure doesn't affect forwarding. Can scale independently.
  Cons: Extra hardware. But this is the right answer 99% of the time.

Design 3: Hierarchical RR (regional RRs → top-level RR)
  Pros: Scales to massive networks (tier-1 carriers)
  Cons: Adds path diversity risk. Watch for suboptimal routing via CLUSTER_LIST.
```

**RR and VPN path selection:**

A subtle but important issue: the RR selects the best path for each VPNv4 prefix and only advertises that best path to clients. If two PEs advertise the same VPNv4 prefix (e.g., dual-homed customer), the RR picks one based on standard BGP best-path selection.

This can cause suboptimal forwarding if the RR picks a path that's closest to itself but far from the receiving PE. Solutions:

1. **ADD-PATH for VPNv4**: RR advertises multiple paths. Receiving PE picks the best for itself. This is the modern answer. Both IOS-XR and Junos support it.
2. **Different RDs per PE**: If PE1 uses RD 65000:1 and PE2 uses RD 65000:2, the RR sees two different VPNv4 prefixes and advertises both. Classic workaround, but wastes RIB space.
3. **BGP Optimal Route Reflection (ORR, RFC 9107)**: RR computes best path from the client's perspective using the client's IGP cost. Supported on IOS-XR (`optimal-route-reflection <group-name>` under VPNv4 address-family) and Junos (`optimal-route-reflection` under the BGP group).

### VPNv4 vs VPNv6 vs VPN-All

| Feature | VPNv4 | VPNv6 | Notes |
|---------|-------|-------|-------|
| Customer address family | IPv4 | IPv6 | |
| AFI/SAFI | 1/128 | 2/128 | |
| RD size | 8 bytes | 8 bytes | Same RD for both |
| NLRI size | Label(3) + RD(8) + IPv4(≤4) = ≤15 bytes | Label(3) + RD(8) + IPv6(≤16) = ≤27 bytes | VPNv6 NLRIs are larger |
| Label encoding | In NLRI | In NLRI | Same mechanism |
| RT encoding | Extended community | Extended community | Same RTs can import both |
| Next-hop in UPDATE | 12 bytes (RD-of-zeros + IPv4) | 24 bytes (RD-of-zeros + IPv6) | 16-byte IPv4-mapped form only with IPv6 BGP sessions (RFC 8950) |
| Typical deployment | Universal | Growing, 6PE/6VPE | |

**6VPE (RFC 4659)**: Carries IPv6 VPN routes over an IPv4 MPLS core. The VPNv6 next-hop is encoded as an IPv4-mapped-IPv6 address, and the transport is standard MPLS. This means you can offer IPv6 L3VPN service without converting your core to IPv6. Most real-world deployments use this — dual-stack VRFs with VPNv4 + VPNv6 over an IPv4 MPLS backbone.

**VPN-All (unified address family)**: Some vendors support a single BGP session that negotiates both VPNv4 and VPNv6 simultaneously. IOS-XR calls this `vpnv4 unicast` + `vpnv6 unicast` under the same neighbor. Junos handles this naturally — you just enable both families under the `inet-vpn` and `inet6-vpn` groups.

### BGP Bestpath for VPN Routes

VPN routes go through standard BGP best-path selection with a few VPN-specific twists:

1. **Weight** (Cisco-specific, local to router)
2. **Local Preference** (default 100, modifiable via PE-CE policy)
3. **Locally originated** (via `network` or redistribution)
4. **AS_PATH length** (shortest wins — but VPN routes between PEs have empty AS_PATH for iBGP)
5. **Origin** (IGP preferred over EGP preferred over Incomplete)
6. **MED** (lower wins — relevant when same prefix from multiple CEs to same PE)
7. **eBGP over iBGP** (not applicable — VPN routes between PEs are always iBGP)
8. **IGP metric to next-hop** (this is where it matters — closest PE wins)
9. **Router-ID** (tiebreaker)

**The critical step for VPN is #8**: IGP metric to the BGP next-hop. When a customer is dual-homed to PE1 and PE2, and both advertise the same VPNv4 prefix to the RR, the receiving PE3 picks the path with the lowest IGP cost to the next-hop PE. This gives you natural closest-exit routing.

**Multi-path**: Both IOS-XR and Junos support eBGP and iBGP multipath for VPN families. If two paths have equal IGP cost to different PEs, both can be installed for ECMP forwarding:

- IOS-XR: `maximum-paths ibgp <n>` under VPNv4 address family
- Junos: `multipath` under the BGP group

### Convergence: BGP PIC for VPN

BGP Prefix Independent Convergence (PIC) pre-programs a backup path in the forwarding plane. When the primary PE fails, traffic shifts to the backup in sub-second time without waiting for BGP reconvergence.

**How it works for VPN:**

1. PE3 receives prefix 10.1.1.0/24 from PE1 (primary) and PE2 (backup)
2. PIC installs both in the forwarding table — PE1 as primary, PE2 as backup
3. PE1 fails → BFD detects in ~50ms → forwarding switches to PE2 path immediately
4. BGP reconverges at its leisure (~seconds) and confirms PE2 as new best

**Requirements:**
- ADD-PATH or different RDs (PE3 must receive both paths)
- BFD between PEs or PE-to-P for fast failure detection
- Platform support for PIC (IOS-XR 7.x+: PIC is enabled by default for VPN prefixes; Junos: `protection` under the family)

## Configuration

### IOS-XR: Full MP-BGP VPN Configuration

```cisco
! === Route Reflector (dedicated, out-of-path) ===
router bgp 65000
 bgp router-id 10.255.0.254
 !
 ! RT-Constraint: address-family ipv4 rt-filter
 address-family ipv4 rt-filter
 !
 address-family vpnv4 unicast
 !
 address-family vpnv6 unicast
 !
 neighbor-group VPN-CLIENTS
  remote-as 65000
  update-source Loopback0
  address-family ipv4 rt-filter
   route-reflector-client
  !
  address-family vpnv4 unicast
   route-reflector-client
   ! ORR: compute best path from client's IGP perspective
   optimal-route-reflection ORR-PRIMARY
  !
  address-family vpnv6 unicast
   route-reflector-client
   optimal-route-reflection ORR-PRIMARY
  !
 !
 ! Apply to all PE neighbors
 neighbor 10.255.0.1
  use neighbor-group VPN-CLIENTS
  description PE1
 !
 neighbor 10.255.0.2
  use neighbor-group VPN-CLIENTS
  description PE2
 !
 neighbor 10.255.0.3
  use neighbor-group VPN-CLIENTS
  description PE3
 !
!
```

```cisco
! === PE Router — MP-BGP VPN with label allocation tuning ===
router bgp 65000
 bgp router-id 10.255.0.1
 !
 address-family ipv4 rt-filter
 !
 address-family vpnv4 unicast
 !
 address-family vpnv6 unicast
 !
 ! Session to RR
 neighbor 10.255.0.254
  remote-as 65000
  update-source Loopback0
  address-family ipv4 rt-filter
  !
  address-family vpnv4 unicast
   ! Send standard communities + extended communities
   ! Extended communities carry Route Targets
   ! Extended communities (carrying RTs) are sent automatically on iBGP
   ! No send-community config needed for iBGP peers on IOS-XR
   soft-reconfiguration inbound always
  !
  address-family vpnv6 unicast
   soft-reconfiguration inbound always
  !
 !
 ! Per-VRF configuration (VPN address families populated by VRF)
 vrf CUSTOMER-A
  rd 65000:100
  address-family ipv4 unicast
   ! Label allocation mode
   label mode per-vrf            ! Use per-vrf to save label space
   ! Or: label mode per-ce       ! One label per CE
   ! Default: per-prefix         ! One label per route (most precise)
   redistribute connected
  !
  address-family ipv6 unicast
   label mode per-vrf
   redistribute connected
  !
  neighbor 172.16.1.1
   remote-as 65001
   description CE1-CUSTOMER-A
   address-family ipv4 unicast
    route-policy PASS-ALL in     ! Mandatory on IOS-XR eBGP
    route-policy PASS-ALL out
    as-override                  ! Use only if customer has single AS across sites
   !
  !
 !
!

! Route-policy (mandatory on IOS-XR)
route-policy PASS-ALL
  pass
end-policy
!
```

```cisco
router bgp 65000

 !
!
```


### Junos: Full MP-BGP VPN Configuration

```junos
# === Route Reflector (dedicated, out-of-path) ===
protocols {
    bgp {
        group VPN-CLIENTS {
            type internal;
            local-address 10.255.0.254;
            cluster 10.255.0.254;
            family inet-vpn {
                unicast;
            }
            family inet6-vpn {
                unicast;
            }
            family route-target;     # RT-Constraint
            # Optimal Route Reflection (ORR)
            optimal-route-reflection;
            neighbor 10.255.0.1 {
                description PE1;
            }
            neighbor 10.255.0.2 {
                description PE2;
            }
            neighbor 10.255.0.3 {
                description PE3;
            }
        }
    }
}
```

```junos
# === PE Router — MP-BGP VPN ===
protocols {
    bgp {
        group RR-PEERS {
            type internal;
            local-address 10.255.0.1;
            family inet-vpn {
                unicast;
            }
            family inet6-vpn {
                unicast;
            }
            family route-target;     # RT-Constraint
            neighbor 10.255.0.254 {
                description RR1;
            }
        }
    }
}

# === VRF (routing-instance) ===
routing-instances {
    CUSTOMER-A {
        instance-type vrf;

        vrf-target target:65000:100;  # Sets both import and export RT

                    type external;
                    peer-as 65001;

                        description CE1-CUSTOMER-A;
                    }
                }
            }
        }
    }
}
```

```junos
    bgp {
        group VPN-CLIENTS {

                }
```

```junos
    forwarding-table {
        export PIC-POLICY;
    }
}

    bgp {
        group RR-PEERS {
            family inet-vpn {
                unicast {
                    add-path {
                        receive;
                    }
                    protection;      # Enable PIC for VPN routes
                }
            }
        }
    }
}
```

```junos
# === Next-hop resolution — inet.3 behavior ===
# By default, Junos resolves VPN next-hops in inet.3 (MPLS-eligible routes only)
# To also use inet.0 (all routes), add:
routing-options {
    resolution {
        rib bgp.l3vpn.0 {
            resolution-ribs inet.0;     # Fallback to inet.0 if not found in inet.3
        }                               # WARNING: This can cause unlabeled forwarding
    }
}
# DO NOT do this unless you understand the consequences. Unlabeled VPN traffic
# hitting P routers = black hole. This is useful only in specific lab/migration scenarios.
```

### Key Knobs

| Parameter | IOS-XR | Junos | Default | Recommended | Why |
|-----------|--------|-------|---------|-------------|-----|
| Label mode | `label mode per-vrf` | `vrf-table-label` | Per-prefix (IOS-XR), Per-next-hop (Junos) | Per-VRF for >50K routes | Saves label space |
| RT-Constraint | `address-family ipv4 rt-filter` | `family route-target` | Off | On | Reduces VPN route flooding |
| ADD-PATH VPN | `additional-paths send/receive` | `add-path send path-count 2` | Off | On for dual-homed CEs | Enables PIC, better path diversity |
| ORR | `optimal-route-reflection <group>` | `optimal-route-reflection` | Off | On for distributed RRs | Prevents suboptimal routing |
| Soft-reconfig | `soft-reconfiguration inbound always` | Implicit (Junos stores all received routes) | IOS-XR: off, Junos: on | On | Required for policy changes without reset |
| Max prefixes per VRF | `maximum-prefix <n>` (per neighbor) | `prefix-limit maximum <n>` | Unlimited | Set per customer SLA | Prevents route table explosion |

### What Good Looks Like

**VPNv4 route on PE (IOS-XR):**
```
RP/0/RP0/CPU0:PE1# show bgp vpnv4 unicast vrf CUSTOMER-A 10.1.1.0/24 detail

BGP routing table entry for 65000:100:10.1.1.0/24
  Versions: Process 42, Table 12
  Local Label: 24001 (per-vrf)
  Paths: (1 available, best #1)
    Advertised to update-groups: 1
    Path #1: Received by speaker 0
      65001                                          ← CE AS
      10.255.0.2 (metric 100) from 10.255.0.254 (10.255.0.254)
        Received Label 24050
        Origin IGP, localpref 100, valid, internal, best
        Extended Community: RT:65000:100
        Received Path ID 0, Local Path ID 1, version 42
        Originator: 10.255.0.2, Cluster list: 10.255.0.254
```

**Key fields to verify:**
- `Received Label` — The VPN label from the remote PE. Must be non-zero.
- `Extended Community: RT:65000:100` — The Route Target. Must match your VRF import policy.
- `metric` after the next-hop — IGP cost to the remote PE. Lower wins for best-path.
- `Originator` + `Cluster list` — Present when route was reflected. Originator is the actual advertising PE.

**RT-Constraint working (Junos):**
```
user@RR> show route table bgp.rtarget.0

bgp.rtarget.0: 6 destinations, 8 routes
+ = Active Route, - = Last Active, * = Both

10.255.0.1:0:65000:100/96
                   *[BGP] 2d 04:00:00, from 10.255.0.1
                      AS path: I
10.255.0.2:0:65000:100/96
                   *[BGP] 2d 04:00:00, from 10.255.0.2
                      AS path: I
```

This shows PE1 and PE2 both subscribe to RT 65000:100. The RR will only send routes with this RT to those PEs.

## Troubleshooting

### Common Issues

| Symptom | Likely Cause | Fix |
|---------|-------------|-----|
| VPN route received but not installed in VRF | RT mismatch — import RT doesn't match the route's RT | `show bgp vpnv4 unicast <prefix> detail` → check Extended Community. Fix VRF import RT. |
| VPN route has no label | Remote PE isn't allocating labels (misconfigured address-family) | Check remote PE: `show bgp vpnv4 unicast labels`. Ensure VRF is under `router bgp` with `address-family vpnv4`. |
| Next-hop unreachable for VPN route | No IGP/LDP/SR route to remote PE loopback | IOS-XR: `show route <next-hop>`. Junos: `show route table inet.3 <next-hop>`. Fix IGP or LDP. |
| RT-Constraint enabled but no filtering happening | RR not configured for rt-filter family | Enable `address-family ipv4 rt-filter` on RR AND mark clients as `route-reflector-client` under that family. |
| VPN traffic black-holed in core | Unlabeled VPN packets hitting P routers (no transport label) | Verify transport label: `traceroute mpls` to remote PE. Check LDP/SR is running on all P-P and P-PE links. |
| Asymmetric forwarding for dual-homed CE | RR picking suboptimal path | Enable ORR on RR, or use ADD-PATH to send multiple paths, or use different RDs per PE. |
| VRF routes not advertised to RR | Missing `redistribute` or `network` in VRF, or missing route-policy on IOS-XR | IOS-XR: Check `show bgp vrf CUSTOMER-A ipv4 unicast summary` for Adj-RIB-Out count. Verify route-policy permits. |
| BGP PIC not working | ADD-PATH not enabled, or backup path not received | `show cef vrf <vrf> <prefix> detail` — look for backup path. Enable ADD-PATH receive on PE. |
| Label allocation hitting limits | Per-prefix with too many routes | Switch to per-VRF (`label mode per-vrf` / `vrf-table-label`). Monitor label utilization. |
| VPNv6 routes not resolving | VPNv6 next-hop format issue (needs IPv4-mapped-IPv6 for 6VPE) | Check `show bgp vpnv6 unicast <prefix> detail` → next-hop format. Ensure remote PE advertises correct next-hop encoding. |

### Methodology

1. **Verify BGP session state**: Is the VPNv4/VPNv6 address family negotiated? Check `Neighbor <IP>, AF VPNv4 Unicast: Advertised and Received`.
2. **Verify routes at source PE**: Does the VRF have the customer route? Is it being advertised with correct RD/RT/label?
3. **Verify routes at RR**: Did the RR receive the route? Is it being reflected? Is RT-Constraint filtering it?
4. **Verify routes at destination PE**: Did the PE receive the route? Is the RT imported? Is the next-hop resolved?
5. **Verify forwarding**: Does `traceroute` from CE to CE work? Check label stack at each hop.
6. **Check MTU**: VPN adds 2 labels minimum (transport + VPN) = 8 extra bytes. With SR-TE policies, the stack can be deeper (e.g., 4+ labels = 16+ bytes). Ensure core MTU handles worst-case stack depth.

### War Stories

> **The Phantom VRF**: A large SP migrated from IOS-XE to IOS-XR on their PE routers. After migration, ~30% of VPN routes between IOS-XR PEs and the remaining IOS-XE PEs disappeared intermittently. The VPN routes would appear, then vanish after a few minutes, then reappear.
>
> Root cause: The IOS-XR PEs were configured with RT-Constraint, but the IOS-XE RR didn't support it. The IOS-XR PEs advertised their RT membership. The IOS-XE RR didn't understand the rt-filter AFI/SAFI but didn't reject it — it just silently ignored the capability. Meanwhile, the IOS-XR PEs *thought* RT-Constraint was active and behaved accordingly.
>
> The intermittent behavior was caused by BGP session flaps during the migration. Each time a session restarted, there was a window where the PE received all routes (before RT-Constraint re-negotiation), followed by the RR's default flooding behavior. But soft-reconfiguration interactions caused periodic route withdrawals as the PE "cleaned up" routes it thought it shouldn't have.
>
> Fix: Upgraded the RR to IOS-XR and properly enabled RT-Constraint on both sides. Routes stabilized immediately. Lesson: Never assume capability negotiation works correctly across different platforms. Always verify with `show bgp neighbor <IP> | include rt-filter` on BOTH sides.
