# 9.2 — DWDM Fundamentals

## Overview
> Dense Wavelength Division Multiplexing is the technology that turned a single fiber pair into an 80+ lane highway. It's the reason your 400G wavelengths coexist on the same glass as someone else's 100G lambdas without interference. For IP engineers, DWDM is the black box between your router's coherent optic and the distant end — and when that black box misbehaves, your IGP convergence tuning from Module 2 won't save you. This section opens the box: wavelength grids, amplifiers, ROADMs, and the optical engineering that keeps photons flying straight across continents.

## Theory

### Why Multiplex?

A single-mode fiber can carry signals across the entire C-band (~1530–1565 nm) and L-band (~1565–1625 nm) simultaneously. Without WDM, you'd use one wavelength per fiber — burning through fiber pairs like a teenager through data plans. WDM lets you stack dozens to hundreds of independent channels on one fiber pair.

**Evolution of WDM**:

| Generation | Channel Spacing | Channels (C-band) | Typical Use | Era |
|-----------|----------------|-------------------|------------|-----|
| CWDM | 20 nm | 8–18 | Metro access, enterprise | 2000s |
| DWDM (100 GHz) | 0.8 nm (100 GHz) | 40 | Legacy long-haul | 2000s |
| DWDM (50 GHz) | 0.4 nm (50 GHz) | 80 | Current standard | 2010s |
| Flex-grid (12.5 GHz) | Variable (12.5 GHz slots) | Variable | 400G+, super-channels | 2020s |

**Key insight**: The move from fixed grid to flex-grid isn't just about cramming more channels. Higher baud-rate signals (64+ Gbaud for 400G/800G) need wider spectral slots. A 400G DP-16QAM signal at 64 Gbaud occupies ~75 GHz — it doesn't fit in a 50 GHz slot. Flex-grid allocates exactly what each signal needs.

### The ITU-T Wavelength Grid

The International Telecommunication Union defines the channel plan in **ITU-T G.694.1** (DWDM) and **G.694.2** (CWDM).

#### DWDM Frequency Grid

DWDM uses frequency (not wavelength) as the reference because frequency spacing remains constant through different media, while wavelength shifts.

**Anchor frequency**: 193.1 THz (≈ 1552.52 nm)

**Channel calculation**:
```
f(n) = 193.1 + n × Δf  THz

Where:
  n = channel number (positive or negative integer)
  Δf = channel spacing (0.1 THz for 100 GHz, 0.05 THz for 50 GHz)
```

**Example C-band channels (50 GHz grid)**:

| Channel | Frequency (THz) | Wavelength (nm) |
|---------|-----------------|-----------------|
| C17 | 191.70 | 1563.86 |
| C30 | 192.30 | 1558.98 |
| C40 | 192.80 | 1555.75 |
| C50 | 193.30 | 1551.72 |
| C60 | 193.80 | 1547.72 |
| C80 | 194.80 | 1539.77 |

*The C-band covers roughly channels C17–C80 on the 50 GHz grid, yielding ~80 usable channels after guard bands.*

#### Flex-Grid (ITU-T G.694.1 Amendment)

Flex-grid abandons fixed channel assignments. Instead:

- **Central frequency**: 193.1 + n × 0.00625 THz (6.25 GHz granularity)
- **Slot width**: m × 12.5 GHz (minimum m=1, typical m=4 for 50 GHz, m=6 for 75 GHz)

This lets you allocate a 75 GHz slot for a 400G super-channel while keeping 37.5 GHz slots for 100G signals on the same fiber. The ROADM must support flex-grid to take advantage of this — legacy fixed-grid ROADMs treat everything as 50 or 100 GHz slots.

#### CWDM vs DWDM Decision

| Criterion | CWDM | DWDM |
|-----------|------|------|
| Channel count | 8–18 | 40–96+ |
| Spacing | 20 nm | 0.8/0.4 nm |
| Amplification | Not practical (too wide) | EDFA/Raman (C/L-band) |
| Reach without amplification | ~80 km | ~80 km |
| Reach with amplification | N/A | 1,000–10,000+ km |
| Optics cost | Low (uncooled lasers) | High (cooled, frequency-locked) |
| Temperature control | Not required | Required (TEC in laser) |
| Use case | Campus, metro access, DCI | Metro core, long-haul, submarine |

**Rule of thumb**: CWDM when you need ≤18 channels over <80 km and want cheap optics. DWDM for everything else.

### Optical Amplifiers

Amplifiers are the heartbeat of long-haul DWDM. Without them, signal attenuation (~0.2 dB/km in C-band SMF) would limit reach to ~80 km. With amplification, photons can cross oceans.

#### EDFA — Erbium-Doped Fiber Amplifier

The workhorse. A length of erbium-doped fiber pumped by a 980 nm or 1480 nm laser. Incoming photons stimulate emission of additional photons at the same wavelength, phase, and direction.

**Characteristics**:
- **Gain bandwidth**: C-band (1530–1565 nm), ~35 nm / ~4.4 THz
- **Gain**: 15–30 dB per stage (typical 20 dB for an 80 km span)
- **Gain flatness**: ±1–2 dB across C-band (with gain-flattening filter)
- **Noise figure**: 4–6 dB (fundamental quantum limit ~3 dB)
- **Output power**: +17 to +23 dBm (total across all channels)
- **Polarization dependence**: <0.5 dB (excellent)
- **Speed**: Gain dynamics ~ms (fast enough for protection switching, too slow for packet-level)

**L-band EDFA**: Longer erbium fiber, 1480 nm pump only. Covers 1565–1625 nm. Typically 15–20 dB gain with higher noise figure (~5–7 dB). Used to double fiber capacity when C-band is full.

**Critical concept — ASE noise**: Every EDFA adds Amplified Spontaneous Emission (ASE) noise. ASE accumulates across amplifier chains. After N amplifiers, the Optical Signal-to-Noise Ratio (OSNR) degrades as:

```
OSNR(dB) ≈ P_launch(dBm) - NF(dB) - 10·log10(N) + 58

Where:
  P_launch = per-channel launch power (dBm)
  NF = noise figure per amplifier (dB)
  N = number of amplifiers in chain (assumes identical spans with gain = span loss)
  58 = constant for 12.5 GHz (0.1 nm) reference bandwidth at 193.1 THz
       (derived from: -10·log10(2·h·ν·Bref) where h·ν = 1.28e-19 J, Bref = 12.5 GHz)

For a different reference bandwidth Bref:
  OSNR(dB) ≈ P_launch - NF - 10·log10(N) + 58 - 10·log10(Bref / 12.5 GHz)
```

*Note: This formula assumes all spans are identical (equal loss and gain). For unequal spans, sum the ASE contribution per amplifier individually — each amplifier's noise contribution depends on its gain and noise figure. Real-world OSNR is typically 2–4 dB lower than this ideal calculation due to connector losses, splice losses, ROADM pass-through penalties, and non-ideal gain flattening.*

**Example**: 10 spans, +1 dBm launch, 5.5 dB NF, 12.5 GHz reference bandwidth:

```
OSNR ≈ P_launch - NF - 10·log10(N) + 58

OSNR ≈ 1 - 5.5 - 10·log10(10) + 58
     ≈ 1 - 5.5 - 10 + 58
     = 43.5 dB
```

43.5 dB is plenty for DP-QPSK (needs ~12 dB) or DP-16QAM (needs ~18 dB). Over 30 spans, you'd get ~38.7 dB — still workable. This is why EDFA chains can span thousands of kilometers.

#### Raman Amplification

Instead of a discrete amplifier box, Raman uses the transmission fiber itself as the gain medium. A high-power pump laser (~1450 nm for C-band amplification) is injected backward (counter-propagating) into the span. Stimulated Raman Scattering transfers energy from pump photons to signal photons.

**Characteristics**:
- **Gain**: 10–15 dB (distributed across ~20–40 km of fiber)
- **Noise figure**: Effective NF of -1 to 2 dB (this is an *equivalent* NF referenced to the span input — not a true negative noise figure. It's better than EDFA because distributed gain occurs before the signal fully degrades. Actual value depends heavily on pump power, fiber type, and span length.)
- **Bandwidth**: ~100 nm (tunable by pump wavelength — not limited to erbium's bands)
- **Pump power**: 300–800 mW per pump (multiple pumps for flat gain)
- **Safety concern**: Class 3B/4 laser safety. Pump must shut down if fiber breaks (automatic laser shutdown / ALS)

**When to use Raman**: Long spans (>80 km between amplifier sites), submarine systems, or when you need to squeeze extra OSNR out of a marginal link. Raman is almost always used *with* EDFA (hybrid amplification), not instead of it.

**Hybrid EDFA + Raman**:
```
                    Counter-propagating Raman pump
                    ◀────────────────────────────
┌──────┐     ┌──────────────────────────────┐     ┌──────┐
│ EDFA │────▶│    Fiber span (~80–120 km)    │────▶│ EDFA │
│(boost)│    │   Raman gain in last ~30 km   │    │(pre- │
│      │     │   of span (distributed)       │    │ amp) │
└──────┘     └──────────────────────────────┘     └──────┘
```

The Raman gain "pre-amplifies" the signal before it reaches the EDFA, effectively lowering the system noise figure by 2–4 dB. On a 100-span submarine system, that translates to thousands of kilometers of additional reach.

#### Semiconductor Optical Amplifier (SOA)

Rarely used in line systems (noisy, NF 7–10 dB, nonlinear at high power). Found in:
- Photonic integrated circuits (PICs)
- Wavelength converters
- Access network reach extenders
- Lab/test equipment

### ROADMs — Reconfigurable Optical Add-Drop Multiplexers

If amplifiers are the heart of DWDM, ROADMs are the brain. A ROADM lets you remotely add, drop, or pass-through any wavelength at any node — without manual patch cord changes, without truck rolls, without touching the fiber plant.

#### Why ROADMs Matter

Before ROADMs, adding a wavelength at an intermediate site meant:
1. Demuxing all channels
2. Physically patching the new wavelength into the mux
3. Sending a technician to do this at 2 AM
4. Praying the tech didn't bump an adjacent fiber (spoiler: they did)

With ROADMs, wavelength provisioning is software-defined. An NMS command or NETCONF RPC reconfigures the optical path in seconds.

#### ROADM Architectures

**Degree**: A ROADM direction. A 2-degree ROADM connects two fiber pairs (east/west on a ring). An 8-degree ROADM connects to 8 different directions — typical for a major hub.

```
                    Degree 1 (East)
                    ◀──────────────▶
                         │
             ┌───────────┼───────────┐
             │      WSS/LCoS        │
Degree 4 ◀──┤    Optical Switch     ├──▶ Degree 2
(North)      │      Matrix          │    (South)
             │                      │
             └───────────┼───────────┘
                         │
                    Degree 3 (West)
                    ◀──────────────▶
                         │
                  ┌──────┴──────┐
                  │  Add / Drop │
                  │  (local Tx/ │
                  │   Rx ports) │
                  └─────────────┘
```

**ROADM Generations**:

| Generation | Architecture | Flexibility | Colorless | Directionless | Contentionless |
|-----------|-------------|------------|-----------|---------------|----------------|
| 1st gen | Fixed filters | Add/drop specific λ only | No | No | No |
| 2nd gen | WSS-based | Any λ, any direction | Yes | No | No |
| 3rd gen | WSS + multicast switch | CDC on add/drop | Yes | Yes | Partial |
| 4th gen (current) | Twin WSS or LCoS | Full CDC | Yes | Yes | Yes |

#### CDC-F: The Four Freedoms

Modern ROADMs aim for **CDC-F** — four properties that make wavelength management truly flexible:

- **Colorless**: Any wavelength can be assigned to any add/drop port. No wavelength-specific hardware.
- **Directionless**: Any add/drop port can connect to any degree (direction). No per-degree add/drop banks.
- **Contentionless**: Multiple instances of the same wavelength can coexist in the add/drop structure (from different degrees) without blocking.
- **Flex-grid**: Variable channel widths (12.5 GHz granularity) for mixed-rate signals.

**Why contentionless matters**: In a degree-8 ROADM, you might receive the same wavelength (e.g., 193.1 THz) from multiple degrees. Without contentionless, you can only drop one instance — the others block. With contentionless (typically using multiple parallel WSS modules or MCS arrays), you can drop all instances simultaneously.

#### Wavelength Selective Switch (WSS)

The core component inside a ROADM. A WSS takes a composite DWDM signal and independently routes each wavelength to a different output port (or blocks it).

**Technologies**:
- **LCoS (Liquid Crystal on Silicon)**: Current leader. Programmable pixel array acts as a diffraction grating. Supports flex-grid natively. Typical specs: 1×20 to 1×35 ports, <6 dB insertion loss, <100 ms switching time.
- **MEMS mirror array**: Micro-mirrors tilt to steer wavelengths. Used in earlier WSS designs.

**WSS specifications that matter**:

| Parameter | Typical Value | Why It Matters |
|-----------|--------------|----------------|
| Port count | 1×9 to 1×35 | Determines max ROADM degree + add/drop |
| Insertion loss | 4–7 dB | Budget for amplifier design |
| Channel isolation | >35 dB | Crosstalk between adjacent channels |
| Passband width | 37.5–75 GHz (flex) | Must accommodate signal bandwidth |
| Switching speed | 10–500 ms | Restoration time |
| Passband ripple | <1 dB | Filter concatenation penalty |

#### Filter Concatenation Effect

As a wavelength traverses multiple ROADMs in a network, each WSS narrows the effective passband slightly. After 10+ ROADMs, the passband can narrow by 20–30%, causing signal distortion at the edges. This is called **filter narrowing** or **filter concatenation penalty**.

**Mitigation strategies**:
- Use wider grid spacing (75 GHz for 400G instead of 50 GHz)
- Deploy WSS with flatter passband profiles (LCoS advantage)
- Limit cascade depth (design rule: typically <15–20 ROADM traversals)
- Use coherent DSP equalization to compensate (modern transceivers handle moderate filtering)

### Fiber Types and Impairments

#### Single-Mode Fiber Variants

| Fiber Type | ITU Standard | Dispersion @ 1550 nm | Effective Area | Use Case |
|-----------|-------------|---------------------|---------------|----------|
| G.652.D (SMF-28) | Standard SMF | 17 ps/(nm·km) | 80 µm² | Metro, access, legacy |
| G.654.E (Cut-off shifted) | Ultra-low loss | 20–22 ps/(nm·km) | 112–150 µm² | Submarine, ultra-long-haul |
| G.655 (NZDSF) | Non-zero DSF | 4–8 ps/(nm·km) | 55–72 µm² | Legacy long-haul (1990s–2000s) |
| G.657.A2 | Bend-insensitive | 17 ps/(nm·km) | 80 µm² | FTTH, indoor cabling |

**Why G.654.E for long-haul?**: Lower attenuation (~0.17 dB/km vs 0.20 dB/km for G.652.D) and larger effective area (125 vs 80 µm²). Larger effective area (112–150 µm² vs 80 µm²) reduces nonlinear impairments because the optical intensity per unit area is lower. On a 10,000 km submarine cable, those differences are worth billions.

**Why NOT G.655 anymore?**: It was designed to reduce chromatic dispersion for direct-detect 10G systems. But coherent receivers actually *want* chromatic dispersion to break up nonlinear mixing (four-wave mixing). So the old "advantage" became a liability. Most new builds use G.652.D (metro) or G.654.E (long-haul).

#### Linear Impairments

These scale predictably with distance and can be compensated:

**Attenuation**: Signal loss through the fiber. ~0.2 dB/km at 1550 nm (C-band). Compensated by amplifiers. Non-negotiable — it's physics.

**Chromatic Dispersion (CD)**: Different wavelengths travel at slightly different speeds in the fiber. Causes pulse broadening. Measured in ps/(nm·km). G.652 fiber: ~17 ps/(nm·km) at 1550 nm.

- Legacy fix: Dispersion-Compensating Fiber (DCF) modules — spools of fiber with large negative dispersion. Adds ~0.5 dB/km loss and delays. Mostly eliminated by coherent DSP.
- Modern fix: Coherent DSP compensates electronically. A 400G transceiver can compensate >50,000 ps/nm of accumulated CD — enough for 3,000+ km of G.652 fiber.

**Polarization Mode Dispersion (PMD)**: The two polarization states of light travel at slightly different speeds due to fiber birefringence (manufacturing imperfections, mechanical stress, temperature). Causes differential group delay (DGD).

- Measured in ps/√km. Modern fiber: <0.1 ps/√km. Legacy/aerial fiber: 0.5–2 ps/√km.
- PMD is stochastic — it varies randomly with time and temperature. Cannot be compensated by fixed DCF.
- Coherent DSP tracks and compensates PMD adaptively. Typical coherent receiver handles 30–100 ps DGD.
- **Gotcha**: Aerial fiber (on utility poles) has terrible PMD due to wind-induced stress. This bites operators who lease aerial routes for long-haul — you get 2 ps/√km instead of 0.05 ps/√km, and your 400G coherent link that should work at 2,000 km only works at 500 km.

#### Nonlinear Impairments

These scale with optical power and are the ultimate limit on capacity:

**Self-Phase Modulation (SPM)**: A channel's own intensity modulates its own phase through the Kerr effect. Causes spectral broadening. Worse at higher launch powers.

**Cross-Phase Modulation (XPM)**: Neighboring channels modulate each other's phase. Particularly nasty in DWDM because channels are tightly spaced. Worse with unequal channel powers.

**Four-Wave Mixing (FWM)**: Three channels at frequencies f1, f2, f3 generate ghost channels at frequencies like f1 + f2 - f3. These ghosts can land directly on top of real channels, causing catastrophic interference. FWM is worst in fibers with low chromatic dispersion (G.655/DSF) — this is why zero-dispersion fiber is actually bad for DWDM.

**Stimulated Brillouin Scattering (SBS)**: Acoustic phonons in the fiber scatter light backward when per-channel power exceeds ~10 dBm for CW (unmodulated) light. For modulated DWDM signals, the effective threshold is higher (~15–17 dBm) because modulation broadens the spectrum beyond the ~20 MHz Brillouin bandwidth. Still limits maximum launch power per channel in long fibers. Mitigated inherently by signal modulation, plus intentional signal dithering if needed.

**Stimulated Raman Scattering (SRS)**: Power transfers from shorter wavelengths to longer wavelengths. In wideband C+L systems, this causes significant tilt across the band. Must be compensated by pre-tilting launch powers or per-channel power control.

#### The Nonlinear Shannon Limit

There's a fundamental trade-off: increasing launch power improves OSNR (fights ASE noise) but increases nonlinear impairments. The optimum launch power per channel in a typical long-haul system is around **0 to +2 dBm**. Go higher, and nonlinear penalties eat your gains. Go lower, and ASE noise kills you. This creates a capacity ceiling — the **nonlinear Shannon limit** — that no amount of engineering can exceed for a given fiber type and span design.

For standard G.652 fiber on the C-band with 80 km spans:
- **Practical capacity**: ~12–16 Tbps per fiber pair (80 × 200G or 40 × 400G)
- **With C+L band**: ~25–35 Tbps per fiber pair
- **Theoretical Shannon limit**: ~100+ Tbps per fiber pair (but unreachable with current modulation/coding)

### Optical Layer Management

#### Optical Channel Monitoring (OCM)

An OCM taps a small percentage of the composite signal (~1%) and measures per-channel power, wavelength, and OSNR. Essential for:
- Verifying channel presence/absence
- Per-channel power equalization
- Detecting wavelength drift
- Alarm generation (channel fail, power degradation)

#### Optical Supervisory Channel (OSC)

A dedicated out-of-band wavelength (typically 1510 nm or 1610 nm, outside the C/L-band) that carries:
- Management/control traffic between DWDM nodes
- Telemetry data (amplifier status, power levels, alarms)
- Software download for line system firmware
- Orderwire (voice communication for field techs — yes, this is still a thing)

The OSC survives even when all traffic-carrying wavelengths are down. It's the optical network's management plane.

#### Automatic Power Control

Modern DWDM systems continuously adjust:
- **Per-channel launch power**: Target a specific power spectral density (typically +1 dBm/ch for 50 GHz spacing)
- **Amplifier gain**: Maintain constant gain or constant output power mode
- **Tilt compensation**: Correct for SRS tilt across the C-band
- **Transient suppression**: When channels are added/removed, the surviving channels experience power excursions due to EDFA gain dynamics. Fast gain control (~µs) prevents transient power spikes that could trigger errors.

```cisco
! Configure a 400G coherent DWDM interface (NCS 1004)
controller Optics0/0/0/0
 transmit-power -10            ! Per-channel launch power (dBm) — set by optical design
```

### Key Knobs

| Parameter | Default | Recommended | Why |
|-----------|---------|-------------|-----|
| Launch power | Varies | Per optical design (-1 to +2 dBm typical) | Too high → nonlinear; too low → poor OSNR. Metro/DCI can go to +3; ultra-long-haul may need -1 to 0. |
| FEC mode | Vendor default | cFEC (400ZR interop) or SD-FEC (max reach) | cFEC for 400ZR multi-vendor, SD-FEC for single-vendor max performance |
| Modulation | Auto | Match to distance/OSNR target | DP-QPSK for long-haul, DP-16QAM for metro/DCI |
| CD range | ±50,000 ps/nm | Match to max expected CD | Under-provisioning causes DSP failure at distant ends |
| OSNR alarm | 15 dB | Set 3 dB above modulation threshold | Early warning before errors |
| Amplifier mode | AGC | AGC (constant gain) for most spans | APC (constant power) only for terminal amps |

### What Good Looks Like

**Healthy 400G DP-16QAM link**:
```
Tx Power:         -1.0 dBm      (within design target ±1 dB)
Rx Power:         -14.0 dBm     (within receiver range: -22 to 0 dBm)
OSNR:             25.0 dB       (above 18 dB threshold + 7 dB margin)
Pre-FEC BER:      2.5e-3        (below FEC threshold ~4.5e-3 for cFEC)
Post-FEC BER:     0.0e0         (error-free after FEC correction)
Q-factor:         8.2 dB        (above cFEC threshold)
CD:               2400 ps/nm    (within DSP compensation range)
DGD (PMD):        3.1 ps        (well below 30 ps limit)
Frequency offset: 0.3 GHz       (locked, <1 GHz drift)
```

**Warning signs**:
- Pre-FEC BER > 1e-2: Approaching FEC cliff — investigate immediately
- OSNR < 20 dB (16QAM): Margin erosion — check amplifier chain, connector losses
- DGD > 15 ps: Significant PMD — possible aerial fiber section or bad splice
- Rx power fluctuating > 2 dB: Possible loose connector, micro-bend, or amplifier instability
- Frequency offset > 2 GHz: Laser drift — possible TEC failure

## Troubleshooting

### Common Issues

| Symptom | Likely Cause | Fix |
|---------|-------------|-----|
| Channel absent (no Rx power) | Fiber cut, ROADM port blocked, wrong frequency | Check OSC alarms, verify frequency config, check ROADM provisioning |
| High pre-FEC BER (>1e-2) | Low OSNR, fiber degradation, connector loss | Check amplifier chain, clean connectors, verify launch powers |
| OSNR degradation (gradual) | Amplifier aging, new splice losses, connector contamination | Trend OSNR over weeks; compare to baseline; OTDR test |
| OSNR degradation (sudden) | Fiber event (dig-up, animal chew), amplifier failure | Check per-span loss; OTDR to localize; check amplifier alarms |
| DGD/PMD spikes | Temperature changes on aerial fiber, mechanical stress | Monitor DGD over 24h cycle; consider buried route or PMD compensator |
| Power oscillation | EDFA transient from channel add/drop, ROADM reconfiguration | Check amplifier transient suppression settings; stagger channel additions |
| Wrong wavelength landing | Frequency config mismatch, ROADM cross-connect error | Verify frequency at both ends; check ROADM circuit provisioning |
| Post-FEC errors | Pre-FEC BER near FEC cliff, intermittent impairment | Check for micro-bends, loose connectors, temperature cycling |
| Spectral hole burning | EDFA gain shape changes with channel loading | Re-equalize per-channel powers; update gain-flattening filter profile |

### Methodology

1. **Check the OSC** — Is the optical management channel alive? If not, you have a complete fiber or amplifier failure.
2. **Per-span loss audit** — Compare current span loss to day-one baseline. An increase of >1 dB indicates a new loss event (bad splice, connector, macro-bend). OTDR if needed.
3. **Per-channel power check** — Use OCM data. All channels should be within ±1 dB of target. Outliers indicate ROADM equalization issues or filter narrowing.
4. **OSNR at receiver** — Compare to modulation-format threshold + required margin (typically 3–5 dB margin). If below margin, trace back through amplifier chain.
5. **Pre-FEC BER vs time** — Stable BER below threshold is fine. Trending upward is a canary. Intermittent spikes suggest environmental effects (temperature, vibration on aerial fiber).
6. **Chromatic dispersion** — Should match expected accumulated CD for the route length. Wildly wrong values suggest the signal is taking an unexpected path (ROADM mis-route).
7. **PMD check** — DGD should be stable on buried fiber, variable on aerial. If DGD is high on buried fiber, suspect a splice or cable section with manufacturing defect.
8. **Nonlinear check** — If OSNR is good but BER is high, suspect nonlinear impairments. Try reducing launch power by 1–2 dB. If BER improves, you were in the nonlinear regime.

### War Stories

> **The Vanishing Wavelength**
>
> A Tier-1 carrier deployed 80 channels of 100G DP-QPSK on a new long-haul route. Channel 37 (193.45 THz) worked perfectly during commissioning but disappeared every afternoon around 3 PM and came back at sunset.
>
> Root cause: The route crossed a railroad bridge. Afternoon sun heated the exposed fiber cable on the bridge, causing massive PMD spikes (DGD exceeding 45 ps). The coherent DSP could handle 30 ps — beyond that, it lost lock.
>
> The fix wasn't optical — it was thermal. The carrier installed a sun shield over the cable tray on the bridge. Cost: $4,000. Revenue at risk from the 100G circuit: $180,000/month. The PM who delayed the fix for three weeks because it wasn't in the capital budget got a lesson in risk management.
>
> **Lesson**: Coherent DSP compensates for a lot, but it's not magic. Environmental factors — sun exposure, wind vibration, ice loading — create time-varying impairments that don't show up during midnight commissioning windows. Always test during worst-case thermal conditions, and monitor DGD over full diurnal cycles.

## Design Considerations

### When to Use DWDM (vs Alternatives)

| Scenario | Recommendation | Why |
|----------|---------------|-----|
| <8 wavelengths, <80 km | CWDM | Cheaper optics, no amplification needed |
| 8–80 wavelengths, any distance | DWDM | Standard sweet spot |
| >80 wavelengths | DWDM C+L band | Double capacity without new fiber |
| Ultra-long-haul (>3000 km) | DWDM + hybrid Raman/EDFA | Raman extends OSNR budget |
| Data center interconnect (<80 km) | DWDM with ZR/ZR+ pluggables | Skip external line system entirely |

### DWDM Design Rules of Thumb

1. **Span budget**: Target 20–22 dB loss per span (≈80–100 km of G.652 fiber + connectors + splices). Each span gets one EDFA.
2. **Channel count planning**: Start with C-band 50 GHz grid (80 channels). Add L-band when >60% C-band is lit (plan the amplifier slots early).
3. **Launch power**: Design for 0 to +2 dBm per channel. Higher for short metro spans (less nonlinear penalty). Lower for ultra-long-haul.
4. **OSNR margin**: Design for 5 dB margin above modulation threshold at end of life. Fiber aging, splice degradation, and component aging will eat 1–2 dB over 15–20 years.
5. **ROADM cascade**: Limit to 15–20 ROADM traversals per lightpath. Beyond that, filter narrowing causes unacceptable penalty for 400G+ signals.
6. **Flex-grid**: Mandatory for networks mixing 100G (37.5 GHz slot) and 400G (75 GHz slot). If buying ROADMs today, flex-grid is table stakes.
7. **Amplifier spacing**: Non-uniform spacing is reality (amplifier sites where you can get power and shelter). Design tools optimize launch powers for unequal spans.
8. **Fiber type awareness**: Know what's in the ground. G.655 NZDSF needs different power optimization than G.652. You can't always choose your fiber — especially on leased routes.

### ZR/ZR+ and IPoDWDM

The industry is converging IP and optical layers by putting coherent DWDM optics directly into router line cards:

- **400ZR** (OIF standard): 400G DP-16QAM, ~120 km reach, QSFP-DD/OSFP form factor, interoperable, low power (15–20W)
- **400ZR+** (OpenZR+): Multi-vendor MSA with extended reach (500+ km), supports multiple modulation formats (QPSK/8QAM/16QAM) for distance-rate trade-offs. Some vendors also offer proprietary modes beyond the MSA spec for maximum reach.
- **800ZR**: Emerging — 800G in a single pluggable, optimized for short metro/DCI

**Impact on network design**:
- Metro/DCI: ZR pluggables eliminate external DWDM transponders. Router connects directly to the ROADM or passive mux.
- Long-haul: Still needs external line system (amplifiers, ROADMs, Raman) but transponder may be the router's pluggable.
- Operational model: Optical parameters (frequency, power, modulation) are now configured on the router, not a separate photonic management system. This is a cultural shift for organizations with separate IP and optical teams.
