# 3.4 — BGP Policy & Traffic Engineering

## Overview
> BGP is the only protocol where you can influence routing decisions across autonomous systems you don't control. Traffic engineering with BGP is the art of making traffic flow where you *want* it to flow — not where the protocol's default behavior sends it. In an SP, this means controlling inbound traffic (from the internet to your customers), outbound traffic (from your network to the internet), and internal traffic flows. The tools are attributes, communities, and a deep understanding of how other networks make decisions.


## Key Concepts
> **After this section you will understand:**
> - how BGP sessions form and exchange reachability at provider scale
> - how next-hop, path attributes, and policy drive best-path decisions
> - design tradeoffs for route reflectors, peering models, and scaling
> - how to troubleshoot control-plane versus policy-induced routing issues

## Theory

### The Two Directions Problem

| Direction | Your Control | Primary Tools | Difficulty |
|-----------|-------------|---------------|------------|
| **Outbound** (your network → internet) | Full | LOCAL_PREF, weight | Easy |
| **Inbound** (internet → your network) | Partial | AS_PATH prepend, MED, communities | Hard |

**Why inbound is harder**: You can't set LOCAL_PREF on other people's routers. You can only *hint* at what you want by manipulating attributes they'll see. Whether they honor those hints depends on their policy.

### Outbound Traffic Engineering

You control outbound. Use these knobs in order of preference:

#### 1. LOCAL_PREF (Best Tool)

Set per peer relationship, override per prefix as needed:

```
Customer routes:  LOCAL_PREF 150
Peer routes:      LOCAL_PREF 100  
Transit A:        LOCAL_PREF 90  (preferred transit)
Transit B:        LOCAL_PREF 80  (backup transit)
```

For specific prefix overrides:
```cisco
! Prefer Transit A for 0.0.0.0/0 (default route)
! But prefer Transit B for 203.0.113.0/22 (specific customer DC prefix)
route-map TRANSIT-B-IN permit 10
 match ip address prefix-list CUST-DC-PREFIX
 set local-preference 95           ! Higher than Transit A's 90
route-map TRANSIT-B-IN permit 100
 set local-preference 80           ! Default for everything else
```

#### 2. AS_PATH Manipulation (for multipath/tiebreaking)

```cisco
! Make AS_PATH artificially longer to deprioritize
route-map TRANSIT-B-IN permit 10
 set as-path prepend 65000 65000 65000    ! Add our ASN 3 times
```

**Caution**: This changes AS_PATH length which affects step 4 of best-path. Only useful when LOCAL_PREF is identical (e.g., two paths from same relationship type).

#### 3. Weight (Cisco-Only, Local-Only)

```cisco
neighbor 198.51.100.1 weight 200    ! Prefer this peer locally
neighbor 198.51.100.5 weight 100    ! Over this one
```

Weight is step 1 in Cisco's best-path — overrides everything. But it's local to the router and not propagated. Useful for quick local overrides, dangerous for inconsistency.

### Inbound Traffic Engineering

This is where it gets interesting. You're trying to influence routers you don't operate.

#### 1. AS_PATH Prepending (Most Common)

Make your route look "farther away" through specific upstreams:

```
Your AS: 65000
Transit A: AS 3356 (preferred inbound path)
Transit B: AS 174  (backup — prepend here)

To Transit A: advertise 203.0.113.0/22 (AS_PATH: 65000)
To Transit B: advertise 203.0.113.0/22 (AS_PATH: 65000 65000 65000)
```

Other networks see:
- Via Transit A: `3356 65000` (length 2) ← preferred
- Via Transit B: `174 65000 65000 65000` (length 4)

**Prepending rules of thumb:**
- 1 prepend: Slight preference shift. May not work if other networks have local-pref overriding AS_PATH.
- 2-3 prepends: Usually effective for shifting majority of traffic.
- 4+ prepends: Diminishing returns. If 3 doesn't work, more won't either.
- **Never prepend with someone else's ASN** — this is effectively an AS_PATH manipulation attack and will get you depeered.

**When prepending fails:**
- The other network uses LOCAL_PREF (step 2) which overrides AS_PATH (step 4)
- The other network has hot-potato routing that doesn't care about your prepends
- The other network strips your prepends (some do this)

#### 2. MED (Multi-Exit Discriminator)

Tell your neighbor which of *their* entry points you prefer:

```
You have two peering points with Transit A:
  West Coast: MED 100
  East Coast: MED 200

Transit A should prefer West Coast for traffic to you.
```

```cisco
! West Coast router
route-map TO-TRANSIT-A permit 10
 set metric 100

! East Coast router  
route-map TO-TRANSIT-A permit 10
 set metric 200
```

**MED limitations:**
- Only compared between routes from the *same* neighbor AS (default)
- Your peer may ignore MEDs entirely
- Your peer may have `always-compare-med` which breaks cross-AS expectations
- MED is step 6 in best-path — many things override it

#### 3. Selective Announcement (Powerful but Coarse)

Advertise different prefix lengths to different upstreams:

```
Your space: 203.0.113.0/22

Transit A (preferred): Advertise 203.0.113.0/22 (aggregate only)
Transit B (backup):    Advertise 203.0.113.0/22 + 203.0.113.0/24 + 203.0.113.4/24
```

When Transit A is up, the aggregate covers everything. If Transit A fails, the more-specifics via Transit B attract traffic (longest match wins, always).

**This is your emergency failover mechanism.** The aggregate provides normal-path routing; the more-specifics are a "break glass" backup.

#### 4. BGP Communities for Upstream Control

Many transit providers offer communities to control their LOCAL_PREF, prepending, and distribution:

**Lumen (AS 3356) example communities:**
| Community | Effect |
|-----------|--------|
| 3356:70 | Set LOCAL_PREF to 70 (deprioritize) |
| 3356:80 | Set LOCAL_PREF to 80 |
| 3356:90 | Set LOCAL_PREF to 90 |
| 3356:100 | Set LOCAL_PREF to 100 (default) |
| 3356:123 | Prepend 3x to all peers |
| 3356:666 | Blackhole |

**NTT (AS 2914) example:**
| Community | Effect |
|-----------|--------|
| 2914:410 | Prepend 1x to all peers |
| 2914:420 | Prepend 2x to all peers |
| 2914:430 | Prepend 3x to all peers |
| 2914:666 | Blackhole |

**This is the most effective inbound TE tool** — you're directly controlling the upstream's LOCAL_PREF, which is step 2 in best-path. Check your transit provider's community documentation (usually on their website or PeeringDB).

### BGP Communities in Depth

#### Standard Communities (RFC 1997)
32-bit value, displayed as ASN:VALUE:
```
65000:100  — Our tagging: peer route
65000:150  — Our tagging: customer route
65000:1001 — Region: US-West
65000:2001 — Action: blackhole
```

#### Well-Known Communities
| Community | Value | Meaning |
|-----------|-------|---------|
| NO_EXPORT | 65535:65281 | Don't advertise to eBGP peers |
| NO_ADVERTISE | 65535:65282 | Don't advertise to ANY peer |
| NO_EXPORT_SUBCONFED | 65535:65283 | Don't advertise outside local sub-AS |
| NOPEER | 65535:65284 | Don't advertise to bilateral peers |
| GRACEFUL_SHUTDOWN | 65535:0 | Graceful shutdown (RFC 8326) |
| BLACKHOLE | 65535:666 | Remotely triggered blackhole (RFC 7999) |

#### Extended Communities (RFC 4360)
64-bit, used for VPN Route Targets and Site of Origin:
```
Route Target:    65000:100  (VPN service identifier)
Site of Origin:  65000:1    (loop prevention in multi-homed VPN)
```

#### Large Communities (RFC 8092)
96-bit: ASN:Function:Parameter — designed for 4-byte ASNs:
```
4200000001:1:100  — Private 4-byte ASN example
65000:100:1001    — Function 100, parameter 1001
```

### Community-Based Policy Framework

A well-designed SP community scheme:

```
ASN:RRRR  — Route type (customer/peer/transit)
ASN:1XXX  — Geographic region
ASN:2XXX  — Actions (blackhole, no-export, prepend)
ASN:3XXX  — Customer-defined (pass-through)
```

Example full scheme for AS 65000:

| Community | Meaning |
|-----------|---------|
| 65000:100 | Learned from peer |
| 65000:150 | Learned from customer |
| 65000:80 | Learned from transit |
| 65000:1001 | Region: US-East |
| 65000:1002 | Region: US-West |
| 65000:1003 | Region: Europe |
| 65000:2666 | Blackhole this prefix |
| 65000:2001 | No-export to all peers |
| 65000:2100 | Prepend 1x to all upstreams |
| 65000:2200 | Prepend 2x to all upstreams |

### Remotely Triggered Blackhole (RTBH)

DDoS mitigation via BGP. When a customer IP is under attack:

1. Inject a /32 host route with BLACKHOLE community (65535:666)
2. Set next-hop to a discard interface (192.0.2.1 → Null0)
3. Route reflectors propagate to all edge routers
4. All edge routers drop traffic to the attacked IP at ingress

```cisco
! On the trigger router (or NOC automation):
ip route 203.0.113.50 255.255.255.255 Null0 tag 666

route-map BLACKHOLE-TRIGGER permit 10
 match tag 666
 set community 65535:666 65000:2666
 set ip next-hop 192.0.2.1
 set origin igp

router bgp 65000
 address-family ipv4 unicast
  redistribute static route-map BLACKHOLE-TRIGGER

! On all edge routers:
ip route 192.0.2.1 255.255.255.255 Null0

! If using eBGP blackhole to upstream:
neighbor X.X.X.X send-community both
! Upstream honors BLACKHOLE community → drops at their edge
```

**Advanced: Flowspec (RFC 8955)**
Instead of blackholing all traffic to a destination, Flowspec lets you match on:
- Source/destination IP
- Protocol (TCP/UDP/ICMP)
- Source/destination port
- Packet length, DSCP, fragment flags

Flowspec rules are distributed via BGP (AFI 1, SAFI 133) and installed as ACL/PBR rules on edge routers. This enables *surgical* DDoS mitigation — drop the attack traffic, keep the legitimate traffic.

### BGP Multipath / ECMP

Load-balance across multiple BGP paths:

```cisco
! IOS-XE: Enable BGP multipath
router bgp 65000
 address-family ipv4 unicast
  maximum-paths 8                    ! eBGP multipath (same AS)
  maximum-paths ibgp 8              ! iBGP multipath
  bgp bestpath as-path multipath-relax   ! Allow multipath across different ASes
```

```junos
# Junos: Enable BGP multipath
routing-options {
    forwarding-table {
        export PFE-LB;
    }
}
policy-options {
    policy-statement PFE-LB {
        then {
            load-balance per-packet;  # Actually per-flow (hash-based)
        }
    }
}
protocols {
    bgp {
        group EBGP {
            multipath {
                multiple-as;          # Multipath across different ASes
            }
        }
    }
}
```

**Key considerations:**
- `multipath-relax` / `multiple-as`: Allows ECMP across paths from *different* neighbor ASes. Without this, only paths from the same AS can multipath.
- Hash-based load balancing: Per-flow, not per-packet. Check hash algorithm (5-tuple is standard).
- Unequal bandwidth links: Use BGP link-bandwidth extended community (draft-ietf-idr-link-bandwidth) for weighted ECMP.

### Conditional Route Advertisement

Advertise or withdraw routes based on availability of other routes:

```cisco
! Only advertise default route to customers if we have a full table from transit
router bgp 65000
 address-family ipv4 unicast
  neighbor 198.51.100.9 advertise-map DEFAULT-TO-CUST exist-map HAVE-FULL-TABLE
  !
route-map DEFAULT-TO-CUST permit 10
 match ip address prefix-list DEFAULT-ROUTE
route-map HAVE-FULL-TABLE permit 10
 match ip address prefix-list TRANSIT-SENTINEL
!
ip prefix-list DEFAULT-ROUTE permit 0.0.0.0/0
ip prefix-list TRANSIT-SENTINEL permit 8.8.8.0/24   ! Canary prefix
```

## Configuration

### Complete TE Example: Dual-Transit with Community Control

```cisco
! === Outbound TE: Prefer Transit A, use Transit B as backup ===
!
route-map TRANSIT-A-IN permit 10
 set local-preference 90
 set community 65000:80 65000:1001 additive
!
route-map TRANSIT-B-IN permit 10
 set local-preference 80
 set community 65000:80 65000:1002 additive
!
! Override: Prefer Transit B for specific content provider prefix
route-map TRANSIT-B-IN permit 5
 match ip address prefix-list CONTENT-PROVIDER-X
 set local-preference 95
 set community 65000:80 65000:1002 additive
!
! === Inbound TE: Attract traffic via Transit A ===
!
! To Transit A: clean announcement
route-map TRANSIT-A-OUT permit 10
 match community CUSTOMER-ROUTES
 ! No prepending — this is our preferred path
!
! To Transit B: prepend to make us less attractive
route-map TRANSIT-B-OUT permit 10
 match community CUSTOMER-ROUTES
 set as-path prepend 65000 65000
 ! Also use Transit B's community to lower their LOCAL_PREF for our routes
 set community 174:70 additive
!
! === Customer-controllable communities ===
! Let customers influence OUR outbound behavior
!
route-map CUSTOMER-IN permit 10
 match community CUST-BLACKHOLE
 set community 65535:666 additive
 set ip next-hop 192.0.2.1
 set local-preference 200
!
route-map CUSTOMER-IN permit 20
 match community CUST-NO-EXPORT-PEERS
 set community no-export additive
 set local-preference 150
!
route-map CUSTOMER-IN permit 100
 set local-preference 150
 set community 65000:150 additive
!
ip community-list standard CUST-BLACKHOLE permit 65000:2666
ip community-list standard CUST-NO-EXPORT-PEERS permit 65000:2001
```

### Key Knobs

| Parameter | Default | Recommended | Why |
|-----------|---------|-------------|-----|
| `maximum-paths` | 1 | 4-8 | ECMP across equal-cost BGP paths |
| `multipath-relax` | Off | On | ECMP across different neighbor ASes |
| `bgp bestpath compare-routerid` | Off | On | Deterministic best-path when all else equal |
| `bgp bestpath med always` | Off | Off (usually) | Comparing MEDs across ASes causes instability |
| `bgp dampening` | Off | Off (usually) | RFC 7196 recommends against dampening for most cases |
| Prepend count | — | 1-3 max | More than 3 prepends has no measurable effect |
| RTBH trigger prefix | — | /32 only | Never blackhole anything larger than a /32 |

## Troubleshooting

### Common Issues

| Symptom | Likely Cause | Fix |
|---------|-------------|-----|
| Prepending not shifting inbound traffic | Upstream uses LOCAL_PREF that overrides AS_PATH | Use upstream's control communities instead |
| ECMP not working (single path in FIB) | `maximum-paths` not set, or paths differ in attribute before IGP cost | Check `show bgp X bestpath` for why paths aren't equal |
| Blackhole not working | BLACKHOLE community not honored by upstream, or next-hop not discard | Verify upstream supports RFC 7999, check `ip route 192.0.2.1 Null0` |
| Traffic still flowing after community change | BGP convergence delay, or soft-reconfig needed | `clear bgp X soft out` to re-advertise |
| Customer communities not taking effect | `send-community both` not configured on customer peer | Always enable on all peers |

### Methodology: Verifying TE Is Working
1. **Check what you're sending**: `show bgp neighbor X advertised-routes` — are attributes correct?
2. **Check what peers see**: Use looking glass servers (bgp.tools, RIPE RIS, RouteViews) to see your routes from external perspective.
3. **Check actual traffic flow**: Netflow/sFlow — is traffic using the path you intended?
4. **Check for asymmetry**: Inbound and outbound paths are independently selected. Asymmetry is normal but should be understood.

### War Story: The Community That Backfired

An SP used transit provider communities to set LOCAL_PREF 70 on their backup transit (making traffic prefer the primary transit). It worked perfectly — for outbound. But they also applied the community to routes *advertised to their customers*.

The customers saw the community and, not knowing what it meant, passed it through to their other providers. Those providers had their own meaning for the same community value. One provider treated `65000:70` as "blackhole in Asia Pacific." All traffic from APAC to the SP's customers vanished.

**Fix**: Strip all internal communities at customer-facing eBGP boundaries. Use `set community ... additive` carefully, and always `set comm-list INTERNAL-COMMUNITIES delete` on outbound to eBGP peers.

**Lesson**: Communities are not namespaced. Your `ASN:VALUE` can conflict with another network's community scheme if leaked. Always sanitize at AS boundaries.
