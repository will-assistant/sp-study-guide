Section 1.2: Design Principles.

Three Principles That Govern Every Decision.

SP network design comes down to three principles that permeate every choice — every protocol selection, every hierarchy level, every timer value, every redundancy model.

Scalability — can this design handle 10 times the current load? Convergence — how quickly does the network recover from failure? And fault isolation — does a failure affect only what it should?

These three are not independent. Optimizing one often creates tension with another. Understanding those tradeoffs — and knowing which principle dominates in which context — is what separates SP networking expertise from protocol memorization.

Scalability.

The scalability problem in SP networks: you have a large number of devices, a large number of routes, and a large number of customers. Each dimension must be controlled independently, and they must not multiply against each other.

For route table scalability: BGP routes — roughly 1 million IPv4 prefixes and growing — live only on PEs and route reflectors. P routers need only IGP routes, typically 5,000 to 20,000 even for a large national backbone. MPLS labels scale independently of the routing table — a PE needs one label per remote PE, not per VPN route.

The mathematical insight: the transport label space on a backbone with N PEs needs order-of-N labels, not order-of-routes-times-N. This separation is why MPLS scales where IP-only backbones cannot.

For protocol scalability: IS-IS handles 2,000 plus routers in a single level with less operational friction than OSPF. BGP route reflectors collapse an N-squared iBGP mesh to order-of-N sessions. SR-MPLS eliminates per-tunnel signaling state on transit routers — no RSVP Path and Resv messages in the backbone.

The design rule: whenever you're evaluating a technology choice, ask "what happens at 10 times scale?" If the answer involves an N-squared relationship — full iBGP mesh, spanning-tree convergence, ARP flooding domain — the design will break at scale.

Convergence.

Sub-50 milliseconds is the target for a protected SP backbone. This is not aspirational — it's a contractual requirement in most carrier SLAs.

For Layer 1 and Layer 2 failure detection, BFD detects link failures in 10 to 150 milliseconds depending on platform. Without BFD, IGP hello dead intervals are 30 to 40 seconds. A typical SP deployment uses 10 millisecond intervals times 3, for 30 millisecond detection, or 50 times 3 for 150 millisecond detection.

For pre-computed fast reroute: RSVP-TE FRR uses pre-signaled bypass tunnels. TI-LFA is the modern approach — SR-MPLS computes backup paths using the P/Q space algorithm with no RSVP state needed. Both achieve sub-50 millisecond switchover.

The convergence budget works backwards from the 50 millisecond target: BFD detection at roughly 30 milliseconds, FRR path switchover at roughly 10 milliseconds, and propagation plus processing at roughly 10 milliseconds. Total: approximately 50 milliseconds.

Fault Isolation.

A large SP backbone is a shared medium. A fault in one domain should not cascade to affect others.

For control plane isolation: IS-IS level boundaries prevent flooding from propagating across the backbone. BGP VRF isolation means a PE's VRF routes are not visible to other VRFs or the global table. Route reflector clients do not peer directly with each other.

For data plane isolation: MPLS VPN label stacks ensure customer traffic cannot cross VRF boundaries. EVPN site-of-origin prevents routing loops at multi-homed sites.

The failure isolation cascade test: for any proposed design, ask "if this device fails, what is the blast radius?" Then ask "if two adjacent devices fail simultaneously?" If a two-device failure takes down an entire metro region, the design needs more redundancy.

Section 1.3: SP vs Enterprise — Fundamental Differences.

This section exists because many SP engineers come from enterprise backgrounds, and the mental model transfer is not always clean.

Protocol Selection: Why SP Chooses Differently.

Enterprise networks default to OSPF. SP networks predominantly use IS-IS. IS-IS runs over any Layer 2 including CLNS-only links. It converges more cleanly with 2,000 plus nodes. Both IS-IS and OSPF support Segment Routing, but IS-IS extensions were deployed first. The honest answer: in a greenfield SP deployment, both work. IS-IS is the overwhelming choice because the industry conventions, tools, and interop experience are there.

BGP in enterprise networking is the external routing protocol — it handles internet edge. In SP networking, BGP is the primary mechanism for distributing VPN routes via MP-BGP, running the EVPN control plane, replacing iBGP full mesh with route reflectors, exporting topology via BGP-LS to SDN controllers, and programmatic traffic steering via Flowspec. An SP BGP design must handle tables of 1 million prefixes, 5,000 to 50,000 VRF route imports per PE, and single route reflectors reflecting routes for 300 plus PEs.

MPLS is a requirement in SP networks. Without MPLS, P routers would need full BGP VPN routes — millions of entries. MPLS decouples routing knowledge from forwarding knowledge. P routers only need to know how to forward labels; the routing intelligence lives at the edges.

Convergence requirements differ dramatically. Enterprise acceptable downtime for failures is 2 to 5 minutes. SP acceptable downtime is sub-50 milliseconds for protected paths. Enterprise uptime target is three nines — 99.9 percent. SP uptime target is five nines — 99.999 percent. Five nines means 5.26 minutes of downtime per year. You can't achieve that without BFD and pre-computed fast reroute.

Multi-Tenancy: The SP Design Constraint.

Enterprise networks handle multi-tenancy as an edge case — guest Wi-Fi, PCI segmentation. SP networks are fundamentally multi-tenant. It's the business model.

Every customer L3VPN service lives in a separate VRF on the PE. The VRF has its own routing table, its own forwarding table, its own BGP import/export policy, and uses a Route Distinguisher to make customer routes globally unique.

A PE with 500 enterprise VPN customers has 500 VRFs. Each VRF looks like a separate router to the customer.

Route Targets determine which VRFs import which routes. Hub-and-spoke VPN: the hub imports all spoke RTs; spokes only import the hub RT. Extranet: a shared services VRF exports with a shared RT. Any-to-any: all sites share the same RT.

Section 1.4: Reference Topologies.

Topology 1: Regional ISP Backbone.

A regional ISP serves a geographic area — a single metropolitan region, a state, or a small country. Typical scale: 300 to 800 routers, 50,000 to 500,000 BGP prefixes, 500 to 5,000 L3VPN customers.

IS-IS design: single Level 2 for the backbone P routers. Optional Level 1 areas for metro aggregation zones. Wide metrics. SR-MPLS prefix SIDs on all P and PE loopbacks.

BGP design: 2 route reflectors, geographically distributed. All PEs are RR clients. Internet edge with 2 to 4 upstream transit peers plus IXP peering.

Topology 2: National Carrier.

A national carrier spans a country with multiple major cities and hundreds of regional PoPs. Scale: 2,000 to 20,000 routers, full BGP table at roughly 1 million prefixes, 5,000 to 50,000 L3VPN customers.

IS-IS design: Level 2 for the national backbone. Level 1 per metro area. Level 1/2 boundary at metro AGG routers. Area summarization reduces backbone flooding.

BGP design: 3 to 4 route reflectors with geographic diversity. Unique cluster ID per RR. ADD-PATH for ECMP and backup path distribution.

Topology 3: Tier 1 Global Backbone.

Tier 1 networks — AT&T, NTT, Deutsche Telekom, Telia — have no upstream transit peers. They carry the global internet themselves. Scale: over 20,000 nodes, full global BGP table, hundreds of IXP connections, all six populated continents.

Additional complexity includes cross-continent MPLS over submarine cable systems, multi-AS design with BGP confederations or route-leaking between ASNs, and Inter-AS MPLS using Option A, B, or C — where Option C with iBGP multihop for VPN plus eBGP labeled-unicast for transport is the most scalable approach for Tier 1 networks.

Review Questions.

Question 1: A regional ISP has 800 routers in a single IS-IS Level 2 domain with 45,000 internal prefixes. During a backbone P-router software upgrade, the NOC observes BGP VPN reconvergence taking 4 to 6 minutes for some customer VRFs. IS-IS reconvergence itself completes in 400 milliseconds. What is the most likely cause, and what design change would address it?

Question 2: You are designing a national carrier backbone for a new SP. The sales team is committing five nines uptime SLAs to enterprise customers. What is your failure recovery strategy, and how do you justify the choice between RSVP-TE FRR and TI-LFA?

Question 3: A customer reports intermittent traffic blackholing in their L3VPN. Traffic flows from Site A to Site B but not from Site B to Site A. What is your diagnostic approach?

Question 4: Your NOC receives an alarm — a backbone P router in the New York core has gone offline. Within 100 milliseconds, all backbone traffic has reconverged via TI-LFA backup paths. Two minutes later, 15 percent of BGP VPN routes still show as unreachable on some PEs. What is happening?

Question 5: You are reviewing a proposed SP backbone design that uses OSPF instead of IS-IS. The designer argues both protocols support SR-MPLS and TI-LFA. What is your response?
