Section 1.1: SP Network Architecture Overview.

What a Service Provider Network Actually Is.

A service provider network exists to carry other people's traffic. Not a subtle distinction — it changes everything about how you design, operate, and think about the network.

An enterprise network connects your users to your applications. You control the traffic. You know roughly who's doing what. The network's job is to be invisible.

A service provider network connects other companies' users to other companies' applications. You don't control the traffic. You often don't know what's flowing. The network's job is to be a revenue-generating service that works so reliably that customers never think about it — and to do that for hundreds or thousands of customers simultaneously without any one of them affecting the others.

This is the foundational fact from which everything else in this guide derives.

The SP Business Model.

Service providers sell connectivity. The products vary — dedicated internet access, MPLS VPN, Ethernet private line, cloud interconnect, mobile data transport — but the underlying model is consistent.

First, customers pay for guaranteed performance: bandwidth, latency, jitter, availability. These become contractual SLAs with financial penalties for violation.

Second, revenue is recurring: monthly charges tied to service uptime. Every outage minute has a direct financial cost.

Third, multi-tenancy is the design constraint: thousands of customers share physical infrastructure. They must be completely isolated from each other in the data plane, and the control plane must not leak routing information between them.

A typical Tier 2 SP might have 800 to 3,000 enterprise VPN customers, 50,000 to 200,000 broadband residential customers, 10 to 100 transit peers and CDN customers, and 50 to 300 IX members. All from the same physical backbone.

Scale: The Numbers That Define SP Design.

The scale difference between SP and enterprise isn't linear — it's categorical.

A large enterprise might have 10 to 50 thousand internal routes, zero to 5 VRFs, and 50 to 500 physical nodes. A regional ISP has over 500,000 full BGP routes, 500 to 5,000 VRFs, and 200 to 2,000 nodes. A Tier 1 national carrier has roughly 1 million full BGP routes, 5,000 to 50,000 VRFs, and 2,000 to over 20,000 nodes.

At regional ISP scale, you cannot run OSPF with 900,000 routes in the global table — it would flood the entire backbone with every BGP prefix change. This is why SP networks use MPLS: the backbone routers don't need to know the BGP routes. They just swap labels. BGP routes live only on the PE routers that connect customers.

The convergence numbers also change. Enterprise acceptable outage is minutes, maybe seconds for critical links. SP acceptable outage is sub-50 milliseconds for protected paths and sub-200 milliseconds for full reconvergence. expert-level exams expect you can tune any tier of the network to hit these targets.

The Three-Tier Topology.

Almost every SP backbone — regardless of size — decomposes into three tiers.

The core tier contains P, or Provider, routers. These handle high-speed label switching only. They have no customer-facing interfaces. Typically no customer BGP — just IGP plus MPLS labels.

The edge tier contains PE, or Provider Edge, routers. Customer VRFs live here. They run BGP for VPNv4, VPNv6, and EVPN. They connect to P routers via labeled paths.

The access tier contains CE, or Customer Edge, equipment. This may be customer-managed or SP-managed as CPE. The PE-to-CE protocol is BGP, OSPF, or static depending on the service product.

P routers are the backbone. They see MPLS labels, not IP prefixes. They switch packets at line rate without inspecting them. A P router with 10,000 IGP routes can forward packets for 50,000 customers without any customer routing knowledge.

PE routers are where the complexity lives. Each PE maintains one VRF per customer for complete routing table isolation, runs BGP with route reflectors to exchange VPN routes, pushes MPLS labels onto customer packets as they enter the backbone, and pops labels and routes to the correct VRF as packets exit.
